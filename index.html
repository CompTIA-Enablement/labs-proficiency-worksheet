Part 1 Product Knowledge

Answer each question as you would in a real customer conversation. Responses should be clear, buyer-focused, and free of exam jargon.

Question 1.1
A buyer asks: "What's the difference between a CompTIA Lab and a practice exam?" How do you respond — and why does that distinction matter in a sales conversation?
A strong answer names the distinction clearly and connects it to a tangible buyer benefit — not just a product description.
Question 1.2
A buyer says: "We already have certification study materials — what does adding labs actually change for our learners?" Give a concise 3–4 sentence answer you would use in a real conversation.
Focus on the outcome difference, not a feature list. What does the learner arrive able to do that they couldn't before?
Question 1.3
What makes CompTIA Labs a competitive differentiator — and how do you position that advantage against a buyer who says their team already has access to vendor-specific hands-on training?
Think about vendor-neutrality, job-readiness, risk-free practice, and scalability. Don't disparage the competitor — reframe the value.
Part 2 Customer Application

Select your primary vertical and complete that scenario. Reviewing the others is encouraged for situational awareness.

Scenario 2.1 — Enterprise
Marcus Dean, Director of IT Operations — Meridian Financial Services
Meridian has 80 help desk and desktop support staff. Marcus has been told by his CTO to cut onboarding time for new hires by 30% over the next two quarters. He currently uses a mix of vendor training and certification study materials. His team holds a range of certs but he says the biggest problem is that new hires "know the theory but freeze on basic tickets."
What is your recommended approach — which labs lead the conversation, which differentiator do you open with, and how do you connect labs to Marcus's 30% onboarding reduction goal?
Scenario 2.2 — Government
Col. James Whitfield (Ret.), Director of Workforce Training — Regional Defense Contractor
Colonel Whitfield manages IT training for 150 personnel across three sites. His organization must demonstrate DoD 8140 compliance by end of fiscal year. He's currently evaluating several certification providers and has asked why he should choose CompTIA Labs over a vendor that offers live range environments. His budget is confirmed and procurement can move in 60 days.
How do you respond to his live range comparison — and what's your argument for CompTIA Labs as the right fit for a DoD 8140 compliance mandate?
Scenario 2.3 — Academic — Higher Ed
Dr. Priya Nair, Chair of IT Programs — Lakewood Community College
Dr. Nair is rebuilding her IT program after receiving employer feedback that graduates "lack practical skills." She has budget from a Perkins V grant and is evaluating lab solutions to complement her existing CertMaster Learn licenses. Her concern is implementation complexity — she has four adjunct instructors and limited IT support staff to manage a new platform.
How do you address Dr. Nair's implementation concern — and how do you frame CompTIA Labs as the solution to the employer feedback her program received?
Scenario 2.4 — Academic — K-12
Roberto Sanchez, CTE Director — Westview Unified School District
Roberto oversees CTE programs across six high schools. He wants to add hands-on IT components to his A+ pathway but has no hardware budget and limited teacher technical expertise. He's been told by his superintendent to demonstrate industry alignment and measurable student outcomes by the end of the school year. He's interested but skeptical that a browser-based lab can deliver "real" hands-on experience.
How do you address Roberto's skepticism about browser-based labs — and how do you connect CompTIA Labs to his dual mandate of industry alignment and measurable student outcomes?
Scenario 2.5 — Delivery Partners
Sarah Kim, VP of Curriculum — TechForward Training Group
TechForward delivers instructor-led CompTIA certification training to enterprise and government clients. Sarah is interested in adding labs to their A+ and Security+ programs but is concerned about two things: her instructors don't have lab facilitation experience, and she's worried that adding labs will push her per-learner cost above what her clients will pay.
How do you address both of Sarah's concerns — instructor readiness and cost — and how do you position labs as a revenue and differentiation opportunity rather than a cost increase?

Part 4 Applied Judgment

Select your primary vertical. Complete both scenarios for that vertical. Reviewing other verticals is encouraged.

Scenario 4.1 — Two Stakeholders, One Meeting
Enterprise: Competing Priorities
You're presenting to an Enterprise VP of IT Operations (who cares about reducing onboarding time and escalation volume) and a CISO (who cares about security skills and compliance posture). They have different definitions of value. You have 20 minutes and both need to leave the meeting with a reason to move forward.
How do you structure your lab demo to serve both stakeholders without losing either of them? What's your narrative thread, which labs do you show, and how do you close to satisfy both?
Scenario 4.2 — Late-Stage Objection
Enterprise: Deal at Risk
You're two weeks from closing a 60-seat CompTIA Labs deal with a mid-size enterprise. The training manager calls to say their CFO has asked why they shouldn't just send the team to a 3-day intensive bootcamp — it's cheaper per head and faster to schedule. The deal is at risk.
How do you respond? What's your argument, and how do you reframe the bootcamp comparison without disparaging it?
Scenario 4.1 — Two Stakeholders, One Meeting
Government: Competing Priorities
You're meeting with a Government IT Training Coordinator (who cares about workforce credentialing and skills development) and a Contracting Officer (who cares about procurement compliance, approved vendor lists, and DoD 8140 documentation). Same meeting, different priorities, and the Contracting Officer controls the budget release.
How do you navigate both stakeholders in one conversation — and how do you ensure the Contracting Officer has the compliance framing they need while keeping the Training Coordinator engaged on skills outcomes?
Scenario 4.2 — Late-Stage Objection
Government: Competitor Claim
You're close to closing a government training contract. The procurement officer calls to say a competing vendor has claimed their platform is already fully DoD 8140 approved and that CompTIA Labs is "still working toward it." The deal is now in question and the procurement officer wants clarification in writing within 48 hours.
How do you respond accurately and confidently — without overpromising on accreditation status — and how do you reframe the conversation to keep the deal alive?
Scenario 4.1 — Two Stakeholders, One Meeting
Higher Ed: Competing Priorities
You're presenting to a Department Chair (who cares about curriculum fit, accreditation alignment, and employer recognition) and a Dean of Workforce Development (who cares about grant compliance, enrollment numbers, and student outcomes data). Both need to sign off on adding CompTIA Labs to the program.
How do you frame CompTIA Labs for both stakeholders in one conversation — and what's your close that gives each of them a specific reason to say yes?
Scenario 4.2 — Late-Stage Objection
Higher Ed: Faculty Pushback
A curriculum committee has approved CompTIA Labs in principle. But a faculty member has now sent a memo arguing that students should pursue AWS or Microsoft certifications instead, because "employers recognize vendor certs more than CompTIA." The Department Chair is asking you to respond to the memo before the next committee meeting.
How do you address the vendor cert objection through the Department Chair — without dismissing the faculty member's concern — and what's your argument for why CompTIA Labs is the right complement to, or alternative to, vendor-specific training?
Scenario 4.1 — Two Stakeholders, One Meeting
K-12: Competing Priorities
You're presenting to a CTE Director (who cares about industry alignment, student career outcomes, and Perkins V reporting) and a Director of Technology (who cares about security, platform management, and IT support burden). The CTE Director is enthusiastic; the Director of Technology is skeptical and controls platform approvals.
How do you run the meeting to win the CTE Director's champion role while also converting the Director of Technology from skeptic to approver?
Scenario 4.2 — Late-Stage Objection
K-12: Superintendent Pushback
A CTE Director you've been working with for two months has just told you her superintendent reviewed the proposal and asked why they should invest in CompTIA Labs when "students can learn the same things on YouTube for free." The superintendent has asked for a one-page justification before approving the purchase. The CTE Director is embarrassed and needs your help.
What's your one-page justification argument — and how do you frame CompTIA Labs against free online resources without being dismissive of the superintendent's concern?
Scenario 4.1 — Two Stakeholders, One Meeting
Delivery Partners: Competing Priorities
You're presenting to a Partner's Director of Curriculum (who cares about content quality, instructor readiness, and delivery flexibility) and their Sales Director (who cares about margin, client demand, and competitive differentiation). Both need to agree before labs get added to the catalog.
How do you make the case for CompTIA Labs to both — and how do you structure the conversation so the Sales Director's business case doesn't undermine the Curriculum Director's quality concerns, or vice versa?
Scenario 4.2 — Late-Stage Objection
Delivery Partners: Client Pushback
A partner you've been working with for three months calls to say one of their major enterprise clients is pushing back on the CompTIA Labs addition to their training program. The client says the cert is "too generalist" and they'd rather invest in a platform-specific training track. The partner is now questioning whether to drop labs from the catalog entirely.
How do you keep the partner relationship and the catalog placement — and what's your argument for why vendor-neutral labs and platform-specific training aren't mutually exclusive?
